Welcome To Khulna

Khulna

a charming city in southern Bangladesh, is celebrated for its rich history and breathtaking natural beauty. Known as the gateway to the Sundarbans, the World's largest mangrove forest and home to the majestic Bengal Tiger, Khulna offers unparalleled access to this UNESCO World Heritage Site. The city itself is dotted with historical treasures like the ancient Shat Gambuj Mosque, a stunning 15th-century architectural marvel in nearby Bagerhat, and the serene Khan Jahan Ali's Tomb. The tranquil Rupsha River flows through Khulna, adding to its scenic charm, while its vibrant bazaars and fresh seafood reflect the region’s cultural essence. Khulna is also a hub of craftsmanship, known for its exquisite honey and unique handicrafts. With its lush green surroundings, historical wonders, and warm hospitality, Khulna offers a perfect blend of natural and cultural delights for visitors.

Sundarban

Sundarban, located in the southern part of Bangladesh and extending into the West Bengal region of India, is the largest mangrove forest in the world. This UNESCO World Heritage Site, covering around 10,000 square kilometers, is known for its unique ecosystem and biodiversity, including the endangered Bengal tiger. The Sundarbans hold historical importance as a vital natural resource for local communities, providing livelihoods through fishing, honey collection, and timber. Over centuries, it has also played a strategic role in protecting the coastal areas from storms and erosion. Today, the Sundarbans remain a symbol of conservation efforts, attracting tourists and researchers keen to explore its ecological significance and rich wildlife.

Shat Gombuz Mosque

Shat Gombuj Mosque, located in Bagerhat District, Khulna Division, Bangladesh, is an architectural masterpiece and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built in the 15th century by the Turkish general Ulugh Khan Jahan, the mosque is renowned for its 60 domes, which give it the name "Shat Gombuj" (meaning "Sixty Domes"). The mosque is a fine example of medieval Islamic architecture in Bengal, blending local and Persian influences. Historically, it served as a center for religious and community activities during the Bengal Sultanate. Today, Shat Gombuj Mosque stands as a significant cultural and historical landmark, attracting visitors who admire its unique design and historical importance.

Shilaidaha Rabindra Kuthi Bari

Shilaidaha Rabindra Kuthi Bari, located in Kusumgram, Kumarkhali Upazila, Kushtia District, Khulna Division, Bangladesh, is a historic residence of the famous poet Rabindranath Tagore. Built in the 19th century, the house served as Tagore's summer residence and a place of creativity, where he wrote several of his works, including poems and songs. The location, surrounded by serene nature, greatly influenced his literary creations. The estate was also a hub for cultural activities, hosting gatherings that promoted Bengali literature and art. Today, Shilaidaha Rabindra Kuthi Bari is a museum and a significant cultural site, attracting visitors keen to explore the legacy of the Nobel laureate.

Bagherhat Museum

Bagherhat Museum, located in Bagerhat District, Khulna Division, Bangladesh, is a significant cultural institution dedicated to preserving the history and heritage of the region. Established in 1981, the museum showcases a rich collection of artifacts, sculptures, and relics from the medieval period, including objects related to the Bengal Sultanate. The museum’s exhibits highlight the region's historical importance as a center of trade, culture, and Islamic architecture. It also features displays related to the UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Shat Gombuj Mosque, and other historical landmarks in Bagerhat. Today, Bagherhat Museum serves as an educational hub, attracting visitors who seek to understand the region's rich cultural and architectural legacy.

Michael Madhusudan Dutta House

Michael Madhusudan Dutta House, located in Sagordari, Keshabpur Upazila, Jessore District, Khulna Division, Bangladesh, is the birthplace of the renowned Bengali poet and playwright Michael Madhusudan Dutta. Built in the 19th century, the house is now a museum dedicated to his life and works. Dutta is celebrated for his contributions to Bengali literature, particularly for his epic poem *Meghnad Badh Kavya* and his role in the development of modern Bengali poetry. The house holds historical significance as a center for his early education and literary growth. Today, the museum attracts literary enthusiasts and tourists who come to explore the legacy of this iconic figure in Bengali literature.

Bangabandhu Island

Bangabandhu Island, located in the Bay of Bengal, near Patuakhali District, Barishal Division, Bangladesh, is a historically significant island named after the founding leader of Bangladesh, Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, who is also known as "Bangabandhu" (Friend of Bengal). The island holds cultural importance as a symbol of the country's struggle for independence. It is part of the country's efforts to preserve the memory of its leaders and historical events. Today, Bangabandhu Island serves as a tourist destination, attracting visitors interested in exploring its natural beauty and learning about the legacy of Sheikh Mujibur Rahman in the creation of Bangladesh.

Genocide Torture Archive & Museum

Genocide Torture Archive & Museum, located in Dhaka, Bangladesh, is a solemn memorial dedicated to the victims of the 1971 Bangladesh Liberation War. Established in 1996, the museum preserves the history of the atrocities and human rights violations committed during the war, particularly focusing on the brutal torture and genocide inflicted by the Pakistani military and their collaborators. The museum showcases photographs, documents, and personal testimonies, offering a powerful reminder of the suffering endured by the people of Bangladesh. Today, the Genocide Torture Archive & Museum serves as an educational and historical landmark.

Khan Jahan Ali Mazar

Khan Jahan Ali Mazar, located in Bagerhat District, Khulna Division, Bangladesh, is the shrine of the legendary Sufi saint Khan Jahan Ali, a key figure in the spread of Islam in the Bengal region during the 15th century. Khan Jahan Ali is credited with establishing Bagerhat as a prominent center of Islamic culture and religion, and he is remembered for his role in building the Shat Gombuj Mosque and other significant structures. His mazar (shrine) remains an important pilgrimage site for followers and visitors alike. Today, the site serves as a symbol of spiritual legacy and cultural history, drawing people who honor the saint’s contributions to Islam and local heritage.
